Understanding Salary Sacrifice: A Guide for Employees

Salary sacrifice is a popular arrangement that allows you to exchange part of your salary for non-cash benefits from your employer. This can be a tax-efficient way to pay for things like pension contributions, childcare vouchers, or a company car. This guide explains how it works.

What is Salary Sacrifice?

A salary sacrifice scheme is a formal agreement between you and your employer to reduce your cash salary in return for a non-cash benefit. Because your official salary is lower, you and your employer pay less in National Insurance Contributions (NICs). You may also pay less income tax, depending on the benefit you choose.

How Does It Work?

Let's say your annual salary is £50,000. You agree to sacrifice 5% of your salary (£2,500) into your company pension plan. Your new, official salary becomes £47,500. You pay income tax and NICs on this lower amount, while the full £2,500 goes directly into your pension pot before tax is applied.

Common Salary Sacrifice Benefits:

  • Pension Contributions: The most common form of salary sacrifice.
  • Childcare Vouchers: A popular choice for working parents.
  • Cycle-to-Work Schemes: For purchasing a bike and cycling equipment tax-free.
  • Company Cars: Usually most effective for low-emission vehicles.

What Are the Main Advantages?

  • Tax and NI Savings: You pay less in National Insurance, and often less in income tax.
  • Increased Pension Contributions: More of your money goes into your pension instead of to the tax man.
  • Access to Benefits: It can make certain benefits more affordable.

Things to Consider

While salary sacrifice is often beneficial, a lower official salary can sometimes affect things like mortgage applications, life insurance cover, and certain state benefits. It's important to understand the full implications before you agree to a scheme.
