Gross Pay vs. Net Pay: What's the Difference?

It's simple, but fundamental:

  • Gross Pay: This is your total earnings before any deductions are taken out. It's the number you see on your employment contract or offer letter.
  • Net Pay (Take-Home Pay): This is the amount of money you actually receive in your paycheck after all deductions have been subtracted.

The deductions can be numerous, but they generally fall into a few key categories. Our calculator focuses on the two biggest ones for most people.

The Major Deductions: FICA and Federal Income Tax

1. FICA Taxes (Social Security and Medicare)

FICA stands for the Federal Insurance Contributions Act. It's a U.S. federal payroll tax that funds two major programs:

  • Social Security: This provides retirement, disability, and survivor benefits. For 2023, the employee tax rate is 6.2% on income up to $160,200.
  • Medicare: This funds the hospital insurance program for seniors and people with disabilities. The employee tax rate is 1.45% on all earnings.

These taxes are mandatory and are calculated as a flat percentage of your gross income (with a cap for Social Security).

2. Federal Income Tax

This is a progressive tax, meaning that people with higher incomes pay a higher percentage of their income in tax. The U.S. has a system of tax brackets. For 2023, the brackets for a single filer start at 10% for income up to $11,000 and go up to 37% for income over $578,125. Your filing status (like Single or Married Filing Jointly) determines the specific thresholds for these brackets.
