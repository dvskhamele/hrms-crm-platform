Understanding Overtime: The Basics

Overtime pay is the additional compensation that non-exempt employees must receive for working more than a certain number of hours in a workweek. In the United States, the primary law governing this is the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA).

The FLSA mandates that for any hours worked over 40 in a workweek, an employee must be paid at a rate of at least 1.5 times their regular rate of pay. This is often referred to as "time and a half."

Why Accurate Overtime Calculation is Crucial

  • For Employees: It ensures you are being paid fairly for your hard work. Underpayment of wages is a serious issue, and understanding your rights is the first step to preventing it.
  • For Employers: Incorrectly calculating overtime is one of the most common and costly compliance mistakes a company can make. It can lead to back-pay lawsuits, fines, and damage to the company's reputation.

Beyond the Basics: Common Overtime Questions

Who is eligible for overtime?

Under the FLSA, employees are classified as either "exempt" or "non-exempt." Non-exempt employees are entitled to overtime pay, while exempt employees are not. The exemption status depends on salary level and job duties, not just a job title.

Can my employer give me comp time instead of overtime pay?

For private-sector employers, the answer is generally no. The FLSA requires that overtime be paid in wages, not in compensatory time off. The rules are different for public-sector (government) employees.

Does overtime apply daily or weekly?

The federal FLSA standard is based on a 40-hour workweek. However, some states, like California, have daily overtime rules as well (e.g., over 8 hours in a day).
